    How the model tracks real sites

    The deployed PV model is run over the last ~90 days of real weather for each known site and compared against that site's measured output. This path never runs the weather model, so it isolates PV-model accuracy from weather-forecast error.

      About PV Prospect

      PV Prospect estimates the annual energy yield of a UK solar installation. The result is a typical-year climatological estimate — not a forecast for a specific year, but a long-run average learned from historical data.

      How the model works

      Two neural networks, chained with a physics step, turn a location, panel rating and orientation into an energy estimate:

      1. 1Weather model — predicts the typical-year solar resource.
      2. 2Solar geometry — projects it onto the panel plane.
      3. 3PV model — converts that into electrical yield.

      1 · Weather model

      Given a location and time of year, the first network predicts a typical-year climatology of the solar resource: direct normal irradiance (DNI), diffuse horizontal irradiance (DHI), and ambient temperature. It is trained on Open-Meteo historical data. It takes no day-specific weather inputs (no live cloud cover, no forecast) — it predicts the long-run average for a location and date, which is why the result is a climatology rather than a forecast for a single year.

      2 · Solar geometry

      A deterministic physics step (no learned parameters) converts DNI and DHI into plane-of-array (POA) irradiance for the panel's azimuth and tilt.

      3 · PV model

      The second network predicts daily capacity factor (kW avg / kW peak) from POA irradiance and temperature, then scales by panel rating to give energy. It is trained on generation data from 10 UK systems shared on PVOutput (unaffiliated).

      • A fixed ~0.7 %/year degradation is applied for installation age.
      • An inverter cap is applied to reconstructed hourly power.
      The annual yield estimate carries a minimum ±17 % (1σ) uncertainty, calibrated by leave-one-site-out cross-validation across the 10 training sites. It captures site-to-site variability but excludes weather-year fluctuations and real-world variation in shading, soiling, and roof condition. The training corpus is self-selected by motivated system owners, so estimates are optimistic for an arbitrary installation.
